Building Strong Passwords

  • Length Matters:
    Aim for passwords that are at least 12 to 16 characters long. Longer passwords are exponentially harder to crack.
  • Mix Character Types:
    Combine uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. This mix increases the complexity of your password.
  • Use Passphrases:
    Consider using a series of unrelated words or a sentence that is easy for you to remember but difficult for others to guess. For example, "Blue!Tree7Rainy#Sky" is stronger than a single word.
  • Avoid Common Words and Patterns:
    Do not use easily guessable words, common phrases, or predictable sequences like "12345" or "password." Even substituting letters (like "P@ssw0rd") can be risky if the underlying pattern is obvious.
  • Uniqueness Is Key:
    Use a unique password for each account. This way, if one account is compromised, your other accounts remain secure.
  • Consider a Password Manager:
    A reputable password manager can generate complex passwords and securely store them so you don’t have to remember each one.

What to Avoid

  • Personal Information:
    Avoid using easily obtainable data such as your name, birthday, pet’s name, or simple variations of these details.
  • Reusing Passwords:
    Don’t use the same password across multiple accounts. Reusing passwords means that a breach on one service can put all your accounts at risk.
  • Obvious Patterns or Sequences:
    Steer clear of sequential numbers (like 1234) or patterns (like qwerty) that hackers can quickly try.
  • Outdated or Common Substitutions:
    Many people believe that replacing "a" with "@" or "s" with "$" makes a password secure. However, these substitutions are well-known to attackers and should not be your only line of defense.
  • Storing Passwords Insecurely:
    Avoid writing passwords down on paper or saving them in unsecured digital files. Use encrypted storage or a trusted password manager instead.

Additional Security Practices

  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):
    Whenever possible, add an extra layer of security by enabling MFA. This means even if someone obtains your password, they’ll still need another form of verification to access your account.
  • Keep Software Updated:
    Regularly update your operating system, browser, and any security software. Updates often include patches for vulnerabilities that could be exploited.
  • Be Cautious with Phishing Attempts:
    Always verify the authenticity of emails or messages asking for login information. When in doubt, navigate directly to the official website rather than clicking on links.
  • Monitor Your Accounts:
    Set up alerts where possible, so you’re notified of any suspicious activity or login attempts. This proactive approach can help you respond quickly if something seems off.
